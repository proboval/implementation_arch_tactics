### Motivation & Gap
Software architecture was emerging as an explicit field of study, yet much of common practice led to architectural designs that were "informal, ad hoc, unanalyzable, unmaintainable, and handcrafted." The editorial introduces the field and frames key research directions.

### Contribution
Defines and frames the field of software architecture by articulating four key distinctions (focus of concern, nature of representation, instance vs. style, design methods vs. architectures) and five areas of impact (understanding, reuse, evolution, analysis, management). Introduces seven papers covering ADLs, formal methods, domain-specific architectures, and architectural refinement.

### Challenges
As an editorial from 1995, it describes the field at a nascent stage where formal architectural notations were not yet widely adopted and practical tool support was limited. The challenges identified (informal descriptions, unenforced architectures) remain partially relevant today.

### Key Quotes
> "Software architecture can expose the dimensions along which a system is expected to evolve. By making explicit the 'load-bearing walls' of a system, system maintainers can better understand the ramifications of changes, and thereby more accurately estimate costs of modifications." (p. 3)

### Key Takeaway
The "load-bearing walls" metaphor for architectural decisions directly supports the thesis argument that architectural tactics are the primary mechanism through which architecture influences maintainability -- modifying these structures (via LLM-driven tactics) should have measurable impact on change propagation costs.
